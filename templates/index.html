Rhetorical Dehumanization of ICE Attacks

“Those who govern, govern also through ideas; thus they govern with the consent of the subordinate classes and not principally through their overt coercion” (Hall et al., 1978). Media, like many things, is inseparable from the social world it sets out to capture, instead it directly reproduces society’s structures of order and power by defining what is “news worthy”, what frame it will sit in, and what kind of interpretations should be prompted. News does not have an intrinsic quality that makes it more valuable, what it has instead is power to inform and legitimize ideologies that maintain the social realities imagined by those in charge. For Latinos all over the U.S., rhetoric in the media has had a reality-confirming effect that has molded the way they interact with the world around them and vice versa. Media rhetoric has made a spectacle of Latino trauma, stripping these communities of their personhood. This dehumanization leaves Latine and immigrant families in perpetual limbo, suspended by their positionality in society, creating an image of their place in society as vulnerable. In deciding whose stories to tell and what features matter most, the media commits an injustice, not accidentally, but by design. We will be analyzing the way that media focuses its reporting of ICE activity only in certain regions, which are not representative of broader trends across the U.S., and that when it tells these stories of ICE interference, it focuses on the violence enacted to these communities, and leaves out stories of their resilience, hope, and power. Following Hall, we hope to foster further questioning around what has been excluded, why, and whose questions never appear at all.

Guiding Question

What does it mean when the media chooses to tell stories of damage but not of resilience, does that choice serve the public, or does it serve a system that benefits from keeping Latine and immigrant communities feeling powerless? And what happens when the out-of-the-ordinary becomes routine?

Sources

Hall, Stuart, et al. Policing the Crisis: Mugging, the State, and Law and Order. Holmes & Meier, 1978.
